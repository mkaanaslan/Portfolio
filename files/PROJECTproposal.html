(i) Problem Description
Nowadays, it has been very important to offer optimal prices to the customers for
profit maximization of any kind in monetary system products. Especially when it comes to
the banks and companies who offer loans, different variable costs occur in different loan
types due to the customer's risk of paying back. Since there is a competitive environment, a
trade-off between highly pricing the loans and losing the risky customers is highly
concerning.
Our project has its focus specifically on agricultural loan applications. Agricultural
loan differs from other loans that the institutions offer because the farmers borrow them to
plant their land, which has a yield that can be affected by environmental factors. In other
words, the overall price of the loan that the customer has to pay depends on not only the risk
segment of the customer but also the farming season type, the location of the farming land,
and the plant type.
The profile of customers is an important concept in this project. Some customers only
care about the price and spend time contacting different lenders and comparing the prices
they offer. Therefore, prices need to be low to capture highly price-sensitive customers. But
other customers are more concerned with convenience or other features. This group of
borrowers is willing to accept a higher price from a lender who can save them time or provide
the features they want. These customers can be charged higher prices.
The goal of the project is to maximize the revenue from the agricultural loan given to
different types of customers. To maximize the profit, personalized dynamic pricing will be
used as the pricing strategy. The main inputs of our strategy will be the credibility risk of the
customer, market risk, and competitor risk. Hence, it is important to derive these in a
meaningful manner.
Credibility risk can be quantified by the details of customer profile and loan
requirements. Payability of the previous loans, the characteristics of the products that are
going to be purchased with the loan, potential price after the harvest, geographic and climatic
conditions are some of the details that can be taken into account.
4
Market risk is defined as the possibility that investments experience losses due to
external factors such as exchange rate, decrease or increase in the price of agricultural
products, inflation, etc. Therefore, it is important to measure or predict the market risk in
agricultural areas since it directly affects the profit.
Competitor risk will be another significant element of our dynamic pricing model.
Knowing the offers from other lenders is a key concept and should be considered so that
possible customers wont end up as failures. There are two possibilities after a lender makes
its offer: Success and Failure. To make a profit in the first place, possible customers must be
convinced to accept the offer and result in success.
In this project, the risks described above are going to be extracted from the data
provided and the external data such as economic indicators, weather conditions, geographic
conditions, demographic and economic status of customers. Different kinds of models such as
machine learning algorithms and optimization techniques are going to be applied to predict
the customer risks and to find the optimal loan prices.
(ii) Literature Review
Before studying a project, it is crucial to look up to the literature, previous studies
about the topic in order to get away from similar mistakes that might have been done in past.
In addition, it is a very useful practice to examine what has been done before and maybe get
different perspectives and new ideas from there in terms of shedding light on our work. For
these reasons, several studies have been reviewed.
In the article, Besbes and Zeevi (2015) considered a multiperiod single product
pricing problem with an unknown demand curve under the stationary demand environment
assumption. The sellers goal is finding the optimal price to maximize cumulative expected
revenue by adjusting prices in each period. Although it is a dynamic optimization problem,
the demand curve is not known by the seller. Besbes and Zeevi argued that the demand curve
is modeled with a linear model. The demand curve could be represented with parameters
according to the past pricing data. Then, the optimal price could be found, this strategy is
known as learning and earning.
5
Ban and Keskin (2020), investigated personalized dynamic pricing with learning.
According to them, a personalized demand model could be constructed, and the relation
between the product demand and customer features could be learned through previous sales
observations. The seller should observe the high dimensional feature vector for each
customer and learn the product demand according to these features. Then, the seller could
employ this information to make personalized pricing for each customer and try to maximize
revenue.
Another approach suggested by Arnoud V. den Boer and Bert Zwart (2014) is that
updating the price with a determined interval around the mean of the previously generated
prices. The idea is called controlled variance pricing. The price of the next iteration is
determined by a function of the previous price and the demand against the selling price. At
the end of the paper, the authors show that the parameters of the function approaches the true
values, which makes the price converge to the optimal.
Is it actually possible for a seller to obtain the total information about the demand
curve of his/her products? This is important when it comes to building an individually
dynamic pricing model. Rothschild (1974) clearly revealed in his study that in most cases, the
answer is not positive, which proves that what the demand model actually concerns can never
be learned by the seller even if she/he sticks to the optimal pricing policy. This concept is
later called one of incomplete learning by McLennan (1984). McLennan shows that optimal
learning can be a sellers optimal choice in a model with discounting. Whereas Harrison,
Keskin, and Zeevi (2012) prove that this occurs generally due to myopic decision making.
Besbes and Zeevi (2009) approached this problem by using a classical statistics
framework instead of leaning on dynamic programming. On the other hand, Levina et al.
(2009) present a machine learning approach to dynamic pricing with model uncertainty,
analyzing the case of strategic consumers. There are some extensions of the previously
discussed models. Keller and Rady (1999) investigated pricing when the demand model
changes over time. Bolton and Harris (1999) studied the strategic experimentation including
multiple firms instead of a monopolist.

Zhijun Chen, Chongwoo Choe, Noriaki Matsushima (2020) claim in their study that
customers who are loyal to a brand Y will buy brand X if and only if the price of firm Y is
higher than the price of company X in a market which each firm has a target group and
consumers information is available for both firms. There are two types of price offer which
are the firms uniform prices are publicly accessible for non-targeted customers of firms and
firms personalized prices to their targeted consumers observed only by the targeted
consumer. Customers are divided into passive customers, who are in the target group of firm
X and the uniform price offered by X is not accessible for them, and active customers, who
are targeted by company X but customers are aware of price discrimination. Active customers
take steps to avoid price discrimination and have access also uniform prices offered by X to
their non-targeted. Finally, each consumer makes a purchase decision after observing the
uniform prices and the relevant personalized price. The study focuses on market profit
analysis of price offers for active and passive customers. They concluded that, in both cases,
using more information about customers could make personalized pricing easier to
implement and benefit consumers by increasing competition. A firm tends to ensure that its
personalized price targeted consumers can compete with the rivals poaching price and its
uniform prices will not be chosen by their active-targeted consumers.
By the literature review, it is concluded that in personalized dynamic pricing there are
some key points that should be considered. First of all, it is important to decide how the
demand curve affects the price offers and the relationship between past pricing data and
optimal price in demand should be investigated. Besides the demand curve in the market, the
personalized demand curve is another important point. The personalized demand curve can
be predicted by subjected customer statistics. Determining price intervals can maximize profit
rather than determine an optimal price point to predict how discounts affect the optimal price.
Moreover, It is fact that there are predictions in customers' preferences, however, it is not
possible to claim that they reflect customers exact preferences due to the uncertainty. Finally,
the pricing strategy should include the rivals prices effect since there is a possibility of
customer losses. In this project, the personalized dynamic pricing model will be constructed
by considering the key points in the literature.
